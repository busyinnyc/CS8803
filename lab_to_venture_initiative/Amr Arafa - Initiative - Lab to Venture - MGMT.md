# Initiative Proposal: The Lab-to-Venture (L2V) Framework: A Train-the-Trainer Commercialization Pipeline

**Topic Area:** Organizational Training, Partner Sustainment & Fundraising

## Initiative Overview
This initiative proposes a structured training procedure targeted directly at the management class within the research group. The goal is to provide a standardized framework that empowers current and future managers to train researchers on how to translate their academic work into commercial pitch decks, identify industry partners, and sustain those external relationships over the long term.

Currently, relying on the administrative or management team to handle all external logistics creates a top-heavy bottleneck and puts partnerships at risk of dying out due to semester-to-semester turnover. By shifting to a decentralized, train-the-trainer model, the organization can scale its commercialization efforts and ensure researchers become the stable point of contact for external partners.

## Hypothesis
If the management class is equipped with a standardized "train-the-trainer" commercialization framework, then they can effectively instruct researchers on how to build compelling pitch decks, leverage professional networks, and independently sustain external partnerships. This approach aims to eliminate administrative bottlenecks and prevent relationship decay caused by semesterly management turnover.

## Discovering An Organizational Need

**1. Brainstorm 5 procedures related to the topic area:**
* Training the management class to instruct researchers in value proposition design and pitch deck creation.
* Developing an "on-ramp" procedure for researchers to integrate their professional networks into group projects.
* Establishing communication protocols designating the researcher as the primary long-term point of contact.
* Designing logistics and preparation for a centralized "Pitch Day" event.
* Transitioning active partner relationships smoothly when the semester ends.

**2. How to collect information for this operation:**
* **Reviewing:** Look at past attempts at external collaborations to identify communication breakdowns.
* **Interviewing:** Speak with researchers to map out industry relationships and identify "on-ramp" needs.
* **Observing:** Watch current managers recruit partners to identify inefficiencies.
* **Surveying:** Ask faculty about requirements to ensure collaborations maintain academic project scope.
* **Analyzing:** Review industry standards for technical pitches to build the training curriculum.

**3. Resources needed for information collection:**
* Roster and professional background data of the current research pool.
* Existing project scopes defined by faculty to ensure alignment with academic boundaries.
* Standardized product management frameworks (e.g., value proposition design).
* Historical email logs or CRM data showing previous outreach conversion rates.

**4. Validation of the procedure:**
* **Sustainment Rate:** Tracking the percentage of partnerships surviving academic semester transitions.
* **Network Utilization:** Measuring new partners recruited through researcher networks.
* **Event Execution:** Hosting a Pitch Day event and gathering VC feedback on researcher pitches.

## Appendix A: The Network "On-Ramp" Protocol (Manager-to-Researcher Guide)
**Purpose:** This protocol guides the management class in training researchers to recruit and sustain external partnerships while maintaining faculty project scope.

### Phase 1: Network Identification & Scope Alignment
* **The Network Audit:** Managers conduct 1-on-1 sessions with researchers to map existing industry connections.
* **The Scope Check:** Potential collaborations must be vetted to ensure they do not alter the fundamental project research scope.
* **Determining Engagement Level:** Defining collaboration types, from Pitch Day attendance to long-term project funding.

### Phase 2: Translation & The Pitch
* **Value Proposition Design:** Training researchers to translate academic findings into a commercial narrative, focusing on strategic positioning and differentiation.
* **Pitch Deck Construction:** Using a standardized template to build investor-ready presentations.
* **Mock Delivery:** Facilitating practice runs to anticipate standard industry pushback.

### Phase 3: The Sustainment Structure
* **Establishing the Point Person:** Designating the researcher as the primary person responsible for sustained communication.
* **Decentralizing the Relationship:** Management must step back from direct daily communication to prevent partner confusion.
* **Semester Transition Protocol:** Ensuring all updates and feedback are housed in researcher files to prevent disruption during management turnover.

## Appendix B: The "Pitch Day" Readiness Curriculum & Event Logistics
**Purpose:** Outlines the curriculum for training researchers for live presentations and establishing guidelines for hosting a centralized event.

### Part 1: The Pitch Delivery Training
* **The Mindset Shift (Academic vs. Commercial):** Training researchers to lead with the **Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)** rather than building up to a conclusion.
* **Mastering "PM" Elements:** Ensuring researchers can articulate strategic positioning and differentiation.
* **Navigating the VC Q&A:** Simulating Q&A sessions focused on business metrics and regulatory pathways.

### Part 2: The Mock Pitch & Stress Test
* **The Internal Panel:** Organizing mock sessions where faculty and managers act as venture capitalists.
* **The Evaluation Rubric:** Grading researchers on clarity, time management, and handling commercial objections.

### Part 3: Pitch Day Logistics & The Sustainment Handoff
* **Distributed Invitations:** Researchers are required to invite their own targets identified during the Network On-Ramp.
* **The Post-Event Handoff:** * Researchers, not managers, send follow-up emails to interested VCs.
    * Managers provide templates but actively step out of the communication loop if a VC reaches out to them directly.
