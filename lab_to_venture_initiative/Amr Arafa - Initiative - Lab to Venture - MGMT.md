Initiative Proposal: The Lab-to-Venture (L2V) Framework: A Train-the-Trainer Commercialization Pipeline
Topic Area: Organizational Training, Partner Sustainment & Fundraising
Initiative Overview
This initiative proposes a structured training procedure targeted directly at the management class within the research group. The goal is to provide a standardized framework that empowers current and future managers to train researchers on how to translate their academic work into commercial pitch decks, identify industry partners, and most importantly, sustain those external relationships over the long term.
Currently, relying on the administrative or management team to handle all external logistics creates a top-heavy bottleneck and puts partnerships at risk of dying out due to semester-to-semester turnover. Because the research pool consists of working professionals with their own natural industry relationships, this initiative will also build a clear "on-ramp" for researchers to bring their own networks into the group. By shifting to a decentralized, train-the-trainer model, the organization can scale its commercialization efforts, successfully host investor pitch events, and ensure that the researchers themselves become the stable point of contact for external partners.
Hypothesis
If the management class is equipped with a standardized "train-the-trainer" commercialization framework, then they can effectively instruct researchers on how to build compelling pitch decks, leverage their existing professional networks, and independently sustain external partnerships. This decentralized approach will eliminate administrative bottlenecks, prevent relationship decay caused by semesterly management turnover, and create a scalable pipeline for integrating external VCs and industry collaborators into the research ecosystem.
Discovering An Organizational Need
1. Brainstorm 5 procedures that could be observed within the organization related to the topic area.
* Training the management class on how to subsequently instruct researchers in value proposition design and pitch deck creation.
* Developing an "on-ramp" procedure for researchers to introduce and integrate their existing professional network into the group's projects.
* Establishing communication protocols that designate the researcher as the primary, long-term point of contact to sustain an external partnership.
* Designing the logistics and preparation curriculum for a centralized "Pitch Day" where researchers present directly to invited VCs.
* Transitioning active partner relationships smoothly when the semester ends so external collaborators are not confused by shifting management personnel.
2. How can you collect information in the organization to form a procedure for this operation? 
* Reviewing: Look at past attempts at external collaborations to identify exactly where the communication broke down or became disorganized during semester transitions.
* Interviewing: Speak with the researchers (who are working professionals) to map out their existing industry relationships and determine what kind of "on-ramp" they need to formalize those connections.
* Observing: Watch how current managers attempt to recruit or cold-call partners to identify inefficiencies.
* Surveying: Ask faculty about their specific requirements for external partner involvement, ensuring that any collaboration maintains the faculty as the driver of the project scope.
* Analyzing: Review industry standards for early-stage technical pitches to build the curriculum that managers will use to train the researchers.
3. What resources would you need to consult to collect this information?
* Roster and professional background data of the current research pool.
* Existing project scopes defined by the faculty to ensure proposed collaborations align with academic boundaries.
* Standardized product management frameworks (e.g., value proposition design, strategic positioning) to adapt into the training curriculum.
* Historical email logs or CRM data showing previous cold outreach attempts and their conversion rates.
4. How will you validate that your procedure is correct? 
* Sustainment Rate: Tracking the percentage of external partnerships that successfully survive the transition between academic semesters without becoming disorganized.
* Network Utilization: Measuring how many new industry partners are successfully recruited through the researchers' own internal networks via the newly established "on-ramp".
* Event Execution: Successfully hosting a Pitch Day event and gathering feedback from VCs on the quality of the researchers' self-delivered pitches.
5. What need is your procedure serving? This procedure serves the critical need of decentralizing partnership management to ensure long-term sustainability.
This initiative aims to reduce:
* The organization becoming too top-heavy and reliant on the management class to handle all external logistics.
* The high churn rate of industry partners who become confused or disengaged due to talking to too many different managers across different semesters.
* The underutilization of the researchers' highly valuable, pre-existing professional networks.
6. Who is the intended audience of your procedure?
* Primary: The management class, who will use this procedure to train others.
* Secondary: The researchers, who will receive the training to build pitch decks and sustain relationships.
* Tertiary: External partners and VCs, who will engage in these streamlined, researcher-led collaborations.

Appendix A: The Network "On-Ramp" Protocol (Manager-to-Researcher Guide)
Purpose: This protocol is designed for the management class to train and guide researchers in identifying, recruiting, and sustaining external partnerships using their own professional networks. It ensures a standardized procedure that maintains the faculty's project scope while empowering the researcher to act as the primary, long-term point of contact.
Phase 1: Network Identification & Scope Alignment
The goal of this phase is for the manager to help the researcher identify viable industry contacts that fit within the established academic boundaries.
* The Network Audit: Managers will conduct a brief 1-on-1 session with the researcher to map out their existing industry connections, former colleagues, or corporate affiliations.
* The Scope Check: Any potential external collaboration must be vetted against the core project requirements. Managers must ensure that bringing in this partner does not alter the fundamental scope of the project, as the faculty must remain the sole driver of the research scope.
* Determining Engagement Level: Managers and researchers will define the desired level of collaboration, ranging from low-level engagement (e.g., attending a Pitch Day, targeted career recruitment) to high-level involvement (e.g., long-term project funding or direct research integration).
Phase 2: Translation & The Pitch
The goal of this phase is for the manager to train the researcher on how to communicate the academic project's commercial value.
* Value Proposition Design: Managers will instruct researchers on how to translate their academic findings into a compelling commercial narrative. This includes defining strategic positioning and competitive differentiation that venture capitalists and industry partners actually care about.
* Pitch Deck Construction: Utilizing a standardized template, the manager will guide the researcher in building a concise, investor-ready presentation.
* Mock Delivery: Before the researcher approaches their contact or presents at a centralized Pitch Day, the manager will facilitate a practice run to rehearse the delivery and anticipate standard industry pushback.
Phase 3: The Sustainment Structure
The goal of this phase is to establish the researcher as the permanent face of the collaboration, preventing relationship decay during semester turnovers.
* Establishing the Point Person: Once the initial contact is engaged, the researcher who brought in the relationship is officially designated as the primary point person responsible for sustaining that communication.
* Decentralizing the Relationship: The management class must explicitly step back from direct, day-to-day communication with the external partner. Having too many different administrative people talking to the partner creates confusion and disorganization.
* Semester Transition Protocol: Managers will ensure that all external communications, updates, and feedback ledgers remain housed within the researcher's project files. This guarantees that when the management class turns over at the end of the semester, the external partner experiences zero disruption.



Appendix B: The "Pitch Day" Readiness Curriculum & Event Logistics
Purpose: This appendix outlines the curriculum the management class will use to train researchers for live investor presentations. It also establishes the operational guidelines for hosting a centralized "Pitch Day" event, ensuring the logistical burden is distributed and post-event relationships are immediately sustained by the researchers themselves.
Part 1: The Pitch Delivery Training (Manager-to-Researcher)
The goal of this phase is to break the habit of "academic presenting" and train the researcher to deliver a concise, commercially viable narrative.
* The Mindset Shift (Academic vs. Commercial): Managers will coach researchers to flip their presentation style. Instead of building up to a conclusion (the academic standard), researchers must learn to lead with the "Bottom Line Up Front" (BLUF), immediately stating the commercial value proposition, the problem being solved, and the market size before diving into the underlying science.
* Mastering the "PM" Elements: Leveraging product management principles, managers will ensure the researcher can clearly articulate:
* Strategic Positioning: Where does this technology sit within the current market landscape?
* Differentiation: Why is this solution fundamentally better or more scalable than existing alternatives?
* Navigating the VC Q&A: Managers will conduct simulated Q&A sessions focused on business metrics. Researchers will be trained to answer questions regarding regulatory pathways, go-to-market strategies, and customer acquisition barriers, rather than just defending their technical methodology.
Part 2: The Mock Pitch & Stress Test
The goal of this phase is a mandatory, high-friction rehearsal prior to facing actual external investors.
* The Internal Panel: The management class will organize a mock pitch session where faculty advisors and select managers act as aggressive venture capitalists.
* The Evaluation Rubric: Researchers will be graded on clarity, time management (sticking strictly to the 10-minute pitch window), and their ability to handle commercial objections without retreating into overly dense technical jargon.
* Iterative Refinement: Based on the panel's feedback, the researcher must refine their deck and delivery before being cleared to present at the official Pitch Day.
Part 3: Pitch Day Logistics & The Sustainment Handoff
The goal of this phase is to execute the event efficiently while strictly enforcing the rule that researchers own the resulting relationships.
* Distributed Invitations: While the management class will handle the broad marketing of the event and baseline logistics (room booking, AV setup), researchers are required to actively invite their own targets identified during the "Network On-Ramp" protocol (Appendix A).
* The Matchmaking Environment: The event will be structured to allow immediate, 1-on-1 networking following the presentations.
* The Post-Event Handoff (Crucial): 
* The management class does not send the follow-up emails to interested VCs.
* Managers will provide researchers with follow-up email templates, but the researcher is responsible for sending the message, establishing themselves immediately as the primary point of contact.
* If a VC reaches out to a manager after the event, the manager will formally introduce them to the researcher and actively step out of the communication loop.
